# Coordinate a Non-Code Portfolio

## Who This Is For

Use this guide when GitHub holds manuscripts, editorial notes, research, datasets, policies, teaching material, journalism, publications, or mixed work. You do not need a terminal or a software-project taxonomy.

## Start With One Outcome

Choose a decision that matters now. Examples include finding the source material for one article, seeing how a manuscript relates to editorial notes, checking whether a dataset can be published, or routing one policy update across several repositories.

Ask your agent:

```text
Help me coordinate the repositories involved in this outcome without changing them. Preserve my vocabulary, state what you can actually see, keep private material private, and recommend the smallest reversible next step. Explain why that step is safe.
```

## Describe Purpose in Your Own Words

Say what each repository helps produce. A repository may support several purposes, such as research and publishing, and may have several roles, such as primary source, evidence collection, or publication surface.

Do not decide lifecycle from age or activity alone. A quiet repository may be an important source archive, a completed publication record, or a dataset that must remain stable.

## Example: Research Portfolio

A useful first review might cover:

- The paper or report being produced.
- Source notes and literature evidence.
- Data provenance, license, and privacy.
- Experiments or analysis.
- The publication website or submission package.
- Mirrors, generated outputs, and external dependencies.

Keep dataset identity and provenance intact. Do not call a mirror a duplicate without relationship evidence. Do not publish the repository map when it reveals private research, embargoed work, participants, clients, or unpublished findings.

## Example: Writing and Publishing

A useful first review might cover:

- Manuscript drafts.
- Editorial comments and decisions.
- Source research.
- Images or publication assets.
- Website or distribution output.

Preserve the writer's stages and vocabulary. A small coordination note may be enough to state which repository owns the manuscript, where editorial decisions live, how publication output is produced, and which relationships remain unknown.

## Choose the Smallest Artifact

Use conversation only when the decision does not need persistence. Use one private coordination note or issue when several repositories need a shared outcome. Use a lightweight index only when findability is the real problem.

Consider a structured inventory, automation, catalog, or portal only after repeated evidence shows that the smaller option cannot carry the work and someone accepts maintenance ownership.

## Review Before Sharing

Check the exact artifact before it becomes a public page, issue, discussion, or portal record. Remove private repository identities, account relationships, local paths, credentials, client information, and unnecessary source details.

Ask who owns refresh and correction. A public artifact without an owner can become stale, misleading, and difficult to retract.

## Finish With Five Answers

You should be able to answer:

- What outcome are these repositories supporting?
- What did the agent observe, and what remains unknown?
- Which repository or person owns each next action?
- What must remain private?
- How can the recommended step be stopped or reversed?

Use [the learning path](LEARNING.md) when you want plain-language explanations of least privilege, untrusted content, privacy, evidence, reversibility, and no change.
